Yann LeCun and the Open / Commons AI Debate (2023-2026)

Scope note. This section covers the "AI-as-commons" debate as it bears on the report's central question - compute outside the classic hyperscaler datacenters. We separate (a) LeCun's rhetorical and institutional position, (b) the data-commons movement, (c) genuinely non-datacenter / decentralized training, and (d) public / sovereign compute. Only (c) is literally about non-datacenter hardware; the others are adjacent and frequently conflated in press coverage.

1. LeCun's position: AI as common infrastructure

Yann LeCun (A.M. Turing Award 2018; NYU professor; Meta Chief AI Scientist and head of FAIR until his departure reported in November 2025 and effective around January 2026) is the most prominent industry voice arguing that frontier foundation models should be open and treated as shared public infrastructure rather than proprietary products held by a few hyperscalers. His position is consistent and documentable:

  • The Wikipedia analogy (X / LinkedIn, Dec 2023). "Open source AI foundation models will wipe out closed and proprietary AI models for the same reason Wikipedia wiped out generalist commercial encyclopedia: crowd-sourced human contributions to open platforms can cater to a high diversity of interests, cultures, and languages." (Yann LeCun, post id 1738641767405138223, x.com). This is explicitly a cultural-diversity / commons argument, not a performance argument.
  • Concentration of power (Lex Fridman Podcast #416, 7 Mar 2024). "I see the danger of this concentration of power through proprietary AI systems as a much bigger danger than everything else… a very bad future in which all of our information diet is controlled by a small number of companies." (lexfridman.com transcript).
  • The 2025 read of the field (AI Pulse, 4 Dec 2025). "OpenAI, of course, stopped being open quite a long time ago. Anthropic never was"; Meta "is kind of rethinking its strategy about open source… maybe not as systematic as it used to be"; and "the best open source models at the moment in the LLM world are all Chinese." (officechai.com). LeCun's own diagnosis, then, is that the Western open-weight movement is partly migrating to Chinese labs and to crypto-funded decentralized collectives.

Two 2026 moves turned this rhetoric into institutions:

  • AMI Labs (announced ~9 Mar 2026). After leaving Meta, LeCun co-founded Advanced Machine Intelligence Labs ("ami" = French for "friend"), HQ Paris with hubs in New York, Montreal, and Singapore, focused on world models / JEPA, not LLMs. It raised a $1.03B (890M EUR) seed at a $3.5B pre-money valuation - reported as Europe's largest-ever seed round, co-led by Cathay Innovation, Greycroft, Hiro Capital, HV Capital, and Bezos Expeditions, with strategic backers reported to include Nvidia, Samsung, Temasek, Toyota Ventures, and Bpifrance. Meta did not invest. (TechCrunch; Crunchbase News; LeCun on X).
  • AI Alliance - Project Tapestry (7 Apr 2026). LeCun was named Chief Science Advisor to the AI Alliance (200+ member organizations), which launched Project Tapestry, an open-source platform for distributed, globally federated training of frontier open models in which institutions and nations contribute while "retaining control of their data and the ability to build… sovereign derivative models." (PR Newswire; thealliance.ai/projects/tapestry). This is the cleanest bridge from LeCun's commons rhetoric to non-hyperscaler, sovereignty-preserving distributed compute - though as of mid-2026 it is a platform-building effort, not yet a trained model.

2. The four camps of the "open / commons" debate

Camp Anchor actors What is actually "open" / "out of the datacenter" Compute reality Status
(1) Data / legal commons (datacenter-bound) EleutherAI (Stella Biderman et al.) + U Toronto/Vector, HF, AI2, MIT, CMU, LLNL The dataset and weights are open; the argument is about licensing, not hardware Trained on conventional clusters Real, shipped - Common Pile v0.1 (8TB, 30 sources, June 2025) + Comma v0.1-1T/2T (7B) matching Llama 1/2 (blog.eleuther.ai; arXiv 2506.05209)
(2) Decentralized / non-datacenter training (the real story) Prime Intellect (SF); Nous Research (Psyche on Solana) Training spread across geographically dispersed, often permissionless heterogeneous GPUs Genuinely distributed at small/RL scale; flagships retreat to centralized NVIDIA clusters Real but caveated (see §3)
(3) Public / sovereign compute (state-funded, non-hyperscaler-owned datacenters) EU AI Gigafactories; US NAIRR; NY Empire AI; Public AI Network Owner is public/consortium, not Google/AWS/Azure - but still a datacenter on Nvidia/CUDA Conventional hardware; large public money, slow delivery Real demand, partly aspirational
(4) The LeCun inflection LeCun + AI Alliance + AMI Rhetoric → Project Tapestry (federated) + AMI (commercial, partially open) Tapestry = distributed-by-design (not yet built); AMI = conventional venture Institutional, early

3. Decentralized non-datacenter training - what's real vs. marketing

Prime Intellect (founded 2023; ~$20M raised incl. a $15M seed extension led by Founders Fund, Feb 2025; backers reported to include Andrej Karpathy and Clément Delangue) produced the genuine milestones:

  • INTELLECT-1 (10B) - trained across 14 nodes on 3 continents.
  • INTELLECT-2 (32B, May 2025, Apache 2.0) - the first 32B model trained via globally distributed reinforcement learning, where "anyone can permissionlessly contribute their heterogeneous compute," with an open stack (PRIME-RL, TOPLOC, SHARDCAST) (primeintellect.ai; arXiv 2505.07291).
  • INTELLECT-3 (106B MoE, 12B active, on GLM-4.5-Air; late Nov 2025) - notably trained on a centralized cluster of 512 NVIDIA H200s across 64 nodes over ~2 months. The flagship retreated to conventional hardware (primeintellect.ai blog; implicator.ai). This is the single most important adversarial flag in the cluster: "decentralized" is doing marketing work for the brand while the best model runs on a normal GPU cluster.

Nous Research raised a $50M Series A led by Paradigm (April 2025) at a ~$1B token valuation, and runs Psyche on the Solana blockchain to pool "idle compute from consumer- and industrial-grade GPUs" (its DisTrO optimizer reduces inter-node bandwidth); it ran a 15B test run in Dec 2024 (SiliconANGLE; The Block). The incentive model is explicitly crypto: idle-compute contribution is framed "less as a donation but more as a transaction," and the valuation is token-denominated, not revenue - treat with caution.

4. Public / sovereign compute

  • EU AI Gigafactories. Council Regulation (EU) 2026/150 (InvestAI ~20B EUR; 3-5 sites of ~100,000 chips each; EU covers up to 17% of capex). The informal call (closed 20 June 2025) drew 76 expressions of interest, 60 sites, 16 member states, >230B EUR proposed, >=3M GPUs foreseen (European Commission) - but the formal call was deferred into early/Q2 2026 (Light Reading). Caveat: the planned facilities run on Nvidia/CUDA, so "sovereignty" is partial.
  • US NAIRR Pilot. NSF + 14 federal agencies + 28 private/nonprofit partners (Microsoft $20M Azure credits; NVIDIA $30M incl. $24M DGX access), 400+ researchers connected, transitioning to a permanent NAIRR Operations Center under NSF 25-546 (Sept 2025) (NSF; Fierce).
  • NY Empire AI. $500M+ public+private (up to $340M state); 10 member institutions; Beta supercomputer at the University at Buffalo on NVIDIA Blackwell, ~11x prior capacity (Gov. Hochul; HPCwire).
  • Public AI Network. Launched a Public AI Inference Utility in Q4 2025 - a free/low-cost "public option" serving open models (Switzerland's Apertus, Singapore's SEA-LION v4) with national system prompts (publicai.substack.com; HF blog). This is inference, not training compute. Naming caution: do not confuse the public-interest publicai.co / publicai.network with the unrelated $PUBLIC web3 token at publicai.io.

5. Bottom line

The appetite for AI compute outside the hyperscalers is real and increasingly well-funded - LeCun has given it intellectual legitimacy, Project Tapestry gives it an architecture, EleutherAI proves the data-commons case scientifically, and public programs have committed hundreds of millions to billions. But the non-datacenter training claim specifically should be read skeptically: the largest open models from the "decentralized" labs are still trained on centralized NVIDIA clusters (INTELLECT-3 on 512 H200s), the crypto-funded valuations are token-denominated, "open source" usually means open-weight-with-restrictions, and the public/sovereign datacenters still run on Nvidia/CUDA. Distributed training is a demonstrated capability at the 10B-32B / RL scale; it is not yet how anyone trains a frontier model.
